Resources for Implicit Bias

Welcome to this resource page on implicit bias (IB). This guide is designed for students who are just beginning to explore how our experiences, social environments, and cultural messages shape the way we think about and interact with others. Here you will find definitions, key concepts such as intersectionality and the Implicit Association Test (IAT), and links to beginner-friendly resources.

What is Implicit Bias?

Implicit bias refers to attitudes or stereotypes that affect our understanding, actions, and decisions without our conscious awareness. These biases develop over time through exposure to media, social environments, and cultural norms.

Because implicit bias operates automatically, it can influence behavior even when a person consciously believes in fairness and equality. For example, bias can affect classroom participation, hiring decisions, healthcare treatment, or how people are perceived in everyday interactions.

Implicit bias is part of a broader concept called implicit social cognition, which explains how perceptions and associations can operate prior to conscious intention or control.

Why it matters: Understanding implicit bias is important because technology, algorithms, and data systems can reflect the same hidden assumptions that exist in society. Recognizing these patterns is the first step toward creating more equitable systems.

Learn more: Understanding Implicit Bias – National Library of Medicine

The Implicit Association Test (IAT)

The Implicit Association Test is a research-based tool that helps individuals explore unconscious associations related to race, gender, age, and other social categories. The test measures reaction times to identify patterns in automatic thinking.

The IAT is not meant to label someone as biased or unbiased. Instead, it is a starting point for self-reflection and awareness. Many students find it surprising to see how quickly their minds make certain associations.

Try it yourself: Project Implicit – Harvard University

Intersectionality

Intersectionality is a concept introduced by legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw. It explains how different aspects of identity—such as race, gender, class, sexuality, and ability—overlap and interact to shape people’s experiences.

Rather than looking at discrimination through a single lens, intersectionality recognizes that individuals may experience multiple forms of disadvantage or privilege at the same time. For example, a person’s experience in a workplace or online space may be shaped by the combination of their gender, race, and socioeconomic background.

Understanding intersectionality is especially important in technology and information systems, where design decisions can unintentionally exclude or disadvantage certain groups.

Resource: Intersectionality and Privilege – University of Edinburgh
